* Exercise 4.9

** Statement
Let \(A,B\in\K^{n\times n}\) be two matrices and let \(p\le n\). The two matrices are defined by blocks as
\[
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1} & A_{1,2}\\
A_{2,1} & A_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
B_{1,1} & B_{1,2}\\
B_{2,1} & B_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix},
\]
with
\[
A_{1,1}\in\K^{p\times p},\quad
A_{1,2}\in\K^{p\times(n-p)},\quad
A_{2,1}\in\K^{(n-p)\times p},
\quad
A_{2,2}\in\K^{(n-p)\times(n-p)}.
\]
Same decomposition seem to hold for \(B\).

(i) Prove that
\[
AB=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1}B_{1,1}+A_{1,2}B_{2,1} &
A_{1,1}B_{1,2}+A_{1,2}B_{2,2}\\
A_{2,1}B_{1,1}+A_{2,2}B_{2,1} &
A_{2,1}B_{1,2}+A_{2,2}B_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

(ii) Show that, if \(A_{2,1}=0\), then
\[
\det A=\det(A_{1,1})\det(A_{2,2}).
\]

(iii) Show that, if \(\det A_{1,1}\ne0\), then
\[
\det A=\det(A_{1,1})
\det(A_{2,2}-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}A_{1,2}).
\]
Indication: The matrix
\[
M=
\begin{pmatrix}
\Id_p&0\\
-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}&\Id_{n-p}
\end{pmatrix}
\]
may be of help.

** Solution

Let \(A,B\in\K^{n\times n}\), \(p\le n\). Two matrices are defined by blocks as
\[
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1} & A_{1,2}\\
A_{2,1} & A_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix},
\qquad
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
B_{1,1} & B_{1,2}\\
B_{2,1} & B_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
With
\[
A_{1,1}\in\K^{p\times p},\quad
A_{1,2}\in\K^{p\times(n-p)},
\]
\[
A_{2,1}\in\K^{(n-p)\times p},\quad
A_{2,2}\in\K^{(n-p)\times(n-p)}.
\]
Same decomposition seem to hold for \(B\).

*** (i) Prove that
\[
AB=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1} & A_{1,2}\\
A_{2,1} & A_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
B_{1,1} & B_{1,2}\\
B_{2,1} & B_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix}
\]
\[
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1}B_{1,1}+A_{1,2}B_{2,1} &
A_{1,1}B_{1,2}+A_{1,2}B_{2,2}\\
A_{2,1}B_{1,1}+A_{2,2}B_{2,1} &
A_{2,1}B_{1,2}+A_{2,2}B_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Now check dimensions:
\[
\dim(A_{1,1}B_{1,1}+A_{1,2}B_{2,1})
=
(p\times p)(p\times p)+(p\times(n-p))((n-p)\times p)
=
p\times p.
\]
\[
\dim(A_{1,1}B_{1,2}+A_{1,2}B_{2,2})
=
(p\times p)(p\times(n-p))+
(p\times(n-p))((n-p)\times(n-p))
=
p\times(n-p).
\]
\[
\dim(A_{2,1}B_{1,1}+A_{2,2}B_{2,1})
=
((n-p)\times p)(p\times p)+((n-p)\times(n-p))((n-p)\times p)
=
(n-p)\times p.
\]
\[
\dim(A_{2,1}B_{1,2}+A_{2,2}B_{2,2})
=
((n-p)\times p)(p\times(n-p))
+
((n-p)\times(n-p))((n-p)\times(n-p))
=
(n-p)\times(n-p).
\]
As we can see, dimensions match, and \(AB\in\K^{n\times n}\).

*** (ii) Show that, if \(A_{2,1}=0\), then
\[
\det A=\det(A_{1,1})\det(A_{2,2}).
\]
If \(A_{2,1}=0\), then
\[
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1} & A_{1,2}\\
0 & A_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
By the Leibniz formula,
\[
\det A=\sum_{\sigma\in S_n}\varepsilon(\sigma)\prod_{i=1}^{n}a_{i,\sigma(i)}.
\]
Since \(\dim(A_{2,1})=(n-p)\times p\), for rows \(i>p\), if \(\sigma(i)\le p\), then
\[
a_{i,\sigma(i)}=0.
\]
So, only permutations that will survive should map all rows \(p+1,\ldots,n\) to columns \(p+1,\ldots,n\),
i.e.
\[
\sigma(\{p+1,\ldots,n\})=\{p+1,\ldots,n\}
\]
and in such case rows \(1,\ldots,p\) will be mapped to columns \(1,\ldots,p\), i.e.
\[
\sigma(\{1,\ldots,p\})=\{1,\ldots,p\}.
\]
Then we split \(\sigma=(\sigma_1,\sigma_2)\), and
\[
\varepsilon(\sigma)=\varepsilon(\sigma_1)\varepsilon(\sigma_2).
\]
Hence
\[
\sum_{\sigma\in S_n}\varepsilon(\sigma)\prod_{i=1}^{n}a_{i,\sigma(i)}
=
\left(\sum_{\sigma_1\in S_p}\varepsilon(\sigma_1)
\prod_{i=1}^{p}a_{i,\sigma_1(i)}
\right)
\left(\sum_{\sigma_2\in S_{n-p}}\varepsilon(\sigma_2)
\prod_{s=p+1}^{n}a_{s,\sigma_2(s)}
\right).
\]
The first sum is \(\det(A_{1,1})\), and the second one is \(\det(A_{2,2})\).
Hence
\[
\det(A)=\det(A_{1,1})\det(A_{2,2}).
\]

*** (iii) Show that, if \(\det A_{1,1}\ne0\),
\[
\det A=
\det(A_{1,1})
\det(A_{2,2}-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}A_{1,2}).
\]
Let
\[
M=
\begin{pmatrix}
\Id_p&0\\
-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}&\Id_{n-p}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Since
\[
\det(M)=\det(M^T),
\]
and \(M^T\) is block upper triangular, by previous point
\[
\det(M)=\det(\Id_p)\det(\Id_{n-p})=1.
\]
Let us multiply \(M\cdot A\). By lemma about matrix product,
\[
\det(MA)=\det(M)\det(A)=\det(A).
\]
Then by point (i)
\[
MA=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1}&A_{1,2}\\
-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}A_{1,1}+A_{2,1}
&
-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}A_{1,2}+A_{2,2}
\end{pmatrix}
\]
\[
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{1,1}&A_{1,2}\\
0&A_{2,2}-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}A_{1,2}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
By point (ii),
\[
\det(MA)=
\det(A_{1,1})
\det(A_{2,2}-A_{2,1}A_{1,1}^{-1}A_{1,2})
=
\det(A),
\]
proving desired property.

* Exercise 4.13

** Statement
Let \(A\in\K^{n\times n}\) with \(n\in\mathbb N\), with coefficients \(a_{ij}\), \(1\le i,j\le n\). For each pair of indices \((i,j)\), define
\[
C_{ij}=(-1)^{i+j}\det(A_{ij}^{*}),
\]
where \(A_{ij}^{*}\) is the matrix obtained by deleting row \(i\) and column \(j\).
Then
\[
\adj(A)=
\begin{pmatrix}
C_{11}&\cdots&C_{n1}\\
\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
C_{1n}&\cdots&C_{nn}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

(i) Prove that
\[
A\adj(A)=\det(A)I_n.
\]

(ii) Conclude that when \(A\) is invertible, we have the following formula for the inverse matrix:
\[
A^{-1}=\frac{1}{\det(A)}\adj(A).
\]
What is \(\det(\adj(A))\)?

** Solution

Let \(A\in\K^{n\times n}\) with \(n\in\mathbb N\), with \(a_{ij}\), \(1\le i,j\le n\). For each pair of indices \((i,j)\), we define
\[
C_{ij}=(-1)^{i+j}\det(A_{ij}^{*}).
\]
Then
\[
\adj(A)=
\begin{pmatrix}
C_{11}&\cdots&C_{n1}\\
\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
C_{1n}&\cdots&C_{nn}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

*** (i) Let \(P=A\cdot\adj(A)\).
\[
p_{ii}=\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{ij}C_{ij}
=
\sum_{j=1}^{n}a_{ij}(-1)^{i+j}\det(A_{ij}^{*})
=
\det A,
\qquad
\forall i\in\{1,\ldots,n\}.
\]

*** (ii) \(i\ne k\).
\[
p_{ik}=\sum_{s=1}^{n}a_{is}C_{ks}
=
\sum_{s=1}^{n}a_{is}(-1)^{s+k}\det(A_{ks}^{*}).
\]
But this is exactly the Laplace expansion of a matrix when \(R_i=R_k\). Let
\[
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
a_{11}&\cdots&a_{1n}\\
\vdots&&\vdots\\
a_{i1}&\cdots&a_{in}\\
a_{i1}&\cdots&a_{in}\\
\vdots&&\vdots\\
a_{n1}&\cdots&a_{nn}
\end{pmatrix},
\]
so \(R_i=R_k\), i.e. row \(k\) replaced by row \(i\). Then
\[
\sum_{s=1}^{n}a_{is}(-1)^{s+k}\det(A_{ks}^{*})
=
\sum_{s=1}^{n}b_{ks}(-1)^{s+k}\det(B_{ks}^{*})
=
\det(B)=0
\]
by alternating property of determinant. Hence
\[
p_{ik}=0\qquad \forall i\ne k.
\]
Since
\[
A\adj(A)=\det(A)I_n,
\]
now \(\adj(A)A=\det(A)I_n\) similarly by the same logic as in previous point.

If \(\det A\ne0\), then
\[
A\cdot\frac1{\det A}\adj(A)
=
\frac1{\det A}\det(A)I=I.
\]
Hence
\[
A^{-1}=\frac1{\det A}\adj(A).
\]
Then
\[
\adj(A)=A^{-1}\det(A).
\]
Therefore
\[
\det(\adj(A))
=
\det(A^{-1}\det(A))
=
(\det A)^n\det(A^{-1})
=
\frac{(\det A)^n}{\det A}
=
(\det A)^{n-1}.
\]

* Exercise 5.3

** Statement
Exercise 5.3. (Shift of spectrum)

Let \(A\in\K^{n\times n}\) and \(\alpha\in\K\). Show that \(\alpha I_n+A\) is diagonalizable if and only if \(A\) is diagonalizable. What are the eigenvalues and associated eigenspaces of \(\alpha I_n+A\)?

** Solution

\(\Rightarrow\) Assume \(A\) is diagonalizable. Then there exists a basis \(E\) made of eigenvectors of \(A\),
\[
E=\{e_1,\ldots,e_n\},
\]
and eigenvalues \(\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n\), not necessarily distinct. Then consider
\[
(A+\alpha I_n)e_i
=
Ae_i+\alpha e_i
=
\lambda_i e_i+\alpha e_i
=
(\lambda_i+\alpha)e_i.
\]
Then \(e_1,\ldots,e_n\) are eigenvectors of \(A+\alpha I_n\), and since those vectors are linearly independent, they form an eigenbasis of \(A+\alpha I_n\), with eigenvalues
\[
\lambda_1+\alpha,\ldots,\lambda_n+\alpha.
\]
Hence \(A+\alpha I_n\) is diagonalizable.

\(\Leftarrow\) Assume \(A+\alpha I_n\) is diagonalizable. Then let
\[
B=A+\alpha I_n.
\]
\(B\) is diagonalizable. Let \(b=-\alpha\). Then
\[
B+bI_n=A.
\]
So we reduced our problem to previous implication. Hence \(A\) is diagonalizable.

The eigenspaces are the same, only the eigenvalues shift:
\[
V_{\lambda}^{A}=V_{\lambda+\alpha}^{A+\alpha I_n}.
\]

* Exercise 5.5

** Statement
Let \(A\) be the \(n\times n\) matrix defined by
\[
A=
\begin{pmatrix}
a&1&1&\cdots&1\\
1&a&1&\cdots&1\\
1&1&a&\cdots&1\\
\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
1&1&1&\cdots&a
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Is \(A\) diagonalizable over \(\K\)? If so, obtain the diagonalized form. Use the result of Exercise 4.14 and Exercise 5.3.

** Solution

Using the result of ex. 4.14, consider
\[
B=
\begin{pmatrix}
1&1&1&\cdots&1\\
1&1&1&\cdots&1\\
1&1&1&\cdots&1\\
\vdots&\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
1&1&1&\cdots&1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
Then
\[
A=B+(a-1)I.
\]
Using exercise 4.14 result, i.e.
\[
\det(w-\lambda e_1,\ldots,w-\lambda e_n)
=
(-\lambda)^n+s(-\lambda)^{n-1},
\qquad
s=\sum_{i=1}^{n}w_i,
\]
where here \(w=(1,\ldots,1)^T\) and \(s=n\), hence applying this result to our problem,
\[
\det(B-\lambda I)
=
(-\lambda)^n+n(-\lambda)^{n-1}.
\]
Up to the same sign convention for the characteristic polynomial, this gives the roots
\[
\lambda=n,\qquad \lambda=0.
\]
So, eigenvalues are
\[
\lambda_1=n,\qquad \lambda_2=0,
\]
with
\[
m_n=1,\qquad m_0=n-1.
\]

\(V_0^B\) is exactly the nullity of our matrix. Then by rank-nullity theorem
\[
\dim(V_0^B)=n-1,
\]
and
\[
V_n^B=\operatorname{span}
\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1\\
1\\
\vdots\\
1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}
\quad\Rightarrow\quad
\dim(V_n^B)=1.
\]
Hence algebraic multiplicities match geometric ones. Hence \(B\) is diagonalizable, with
\[
D_B=
\begin{pmatrix}
n&0&\cdots&0\\
0&0&\cdots&0\\
\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
0&0&\cdots&0
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
By ex. 5.3,
\[
A=B+(a-1)I,
\]
and eigenvalues of \(A=B+(a-1)I\) are
\[
\mu_1=n+(a-1)
\]
with
\[
m_{n+a-1}=1,
\]
and
\[
\mu_2=a-1
\]
with
\[
m_{a-1}=n-1.
\]
Then
\[
D_A=
\begin{pmatrix}
n+a-1&0&\cdots&0\\
0&a-1&\cdots&0\\
\vdots&\vdots&\ddots&\vdots\\
0&0&\cdots&a-1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
